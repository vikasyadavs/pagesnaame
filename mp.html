Work History of Alirajpur

A legacy built on vibrant tribal craftsmanship, expansive wood art, and rich agrarian tradelines that have sustained the region for generations.

Tribal Artisans & Crafts

Historically, Alirajpur's economy thrived through its indigenous tribal communities, particularly the Bhils and Bhilalas. Famous for intricate 'Pithora' paintings, handloom weaving, and detailed wooden crafts, self-reliance and artistic commerce defined the foundational workforce.

Agricultural Heritage

Blessed with rolling landscapes and forested plateaus, agriculture has historically employed over 80% of the native population. Traditional farming of minor millets, maize, and pulses formed the absolute bedrock of commercial trading with adjacent districts.

Forestry & Minor Produce

As a heavily forested border district, the ancient economic ecosystem relied immensely on collection and trade of minor forest produce (MFP) such as Mahua flowers, Tendu leaves, and medicinal herbs, anchoring local markets.

Employment Evolution

How the formal and informal employment sectors transformed over decades as connectivity and industrial policy reached Western Madhya Pradesh.

1980s

Agrarian Dominance

Total reliance on traditional agriculture and local barter trade. Formal recruitment setups were minimal, limited purely to core administrative district offices.

2000s

District Autonomy & Public Growth

With Alirajpur being explicitly carved out as a separate district in 2008, a massive wave of public sector employment arose in civil administration, infrastructure development, policing, and schools.

2015+

Private and MSME Diversification

Sustained improvements in highways boosted trade. The private footprint expanded exponentially across retail, agro-processing mills, logistical supply chains, and banking services.
