Work History of the District

A heritage deeply anchored in extensive evergreen forest canopy operations, artisanal woodwork networks, and diverse settlement agriculture.

The Timber Legacy & Forest Utilization

For generations, the economic baseline of the North and Middle Andaman topography was driven by sustainable silviculture management. Major logging frameworks, structural sawmills, and processing cooperatives centered in Chatham, Mayabunder, and Rangat supported a large labor ecosystem. Skilled woodcutters, timber graders, and carpenters formed the core of the island's early workforce.

The Paddy & Cash Crop Frontier

With the arrival of settlement programs from mainland India, areas like Diglipur transformed into productive agricultural zones. The combination of rich soil and high rainfall turned the sub-district into the primary source of rice, vegetables, and cash crops like black pepper and betel nut, supplying the wider archipelago.

Decadal Employment Shifts

Tracking the steady transition from primary forestry work to structured private enterprises and conservation fields.

1980s

The Timber Base Era

Employment focused primarily on logging operations, raw timber transport, and public sawmills. Government-run wood workshops absorbed the majority of local technical school graduates.

2000s

The Conservation Turn

Strict forest conservation laws led to the downsizing of large commercial sawmills. This prompted a major shift of workers from logging into public construction, agricultural production, and plantation management.

2020s

The Eco-Hospitality Frontier

Improved transport links along the Andaman Trunk Road unlocked a boom in sustainable travel. Modern eco-resorts, marine extraction hubs, and cold-storage operations now drive private sector hiring.
